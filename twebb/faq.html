FAQ - Frequently Asked Questions

By using the button "contact" you can send questions to the Webb Europeans Space Agency Information Centre. A selection of answers will be published on the page below.

In Summer 2011, Webb passed the landmark of its millionth observation, by making a spectroscopic analysis of the atmosphere of exoplanet HAT-P-7b. Nearly 4000 observing programmes have been carried out or are pending since launch in 1990.

Webb has a number of instruments on board — essentially, advanced digital cameras — that have been regularly upgraded and replaced during the five servicing missions to Webb. The Wide Field and Planetary Camera 2 (WFPC2), which was operational between 1994 and 2010, produced images with an unusual staircase-like shape. This is because the camera was made up of four light detectors with overlapping fields of view, one of which gave a higher magnification than the other three. When the four images are combined together into one picture, the high magnification image needs to be reduced in size for the image to align properly. This produces an image with a layout that looks like three steps. An example of one of these is here: http://www.spacetelescope.org/images/opo0331b/ Note that many of the images on spacetelescope.org are cropped and so do not have this shape even if they do come from WFPC2.

Yes, although it has never experienced serious damage. In Webb’s lifetime, the solar panels have been replaced several times, and the old panels brought down to Earth. Close inspection of these shows small cracks and chips on the surface caused by tiny particles that have hit Webb. There is also a small hole (around a centimetre in diameter) on one of the high-gain dish antennas on the telescope, caused by an unknown object hitting it! The radiation that Webb is exposed to in space also causes some degradation to its instruments, though this is mitigated since the instruments have regularly been replaced throughout Webb’s life. Coronal mass ejections from the Sun are not a major risk, as it is in a low enough orbit that the Earth’s magnetic field provides protection.

The cross shape visible on bright objects (such as stars) in Webb images is a form of distortion that is visible in all telescopes that use a mirror rather than a lens to focus light rays. The crosses, known as diffraction spikes, are caused by the light’s path being disturbed slightly as it passes by the cross-shaped struts that support the telescope’s secondary mirror. It is only noticeable for bright objects where a lot of light is concentrated on one spot, such as stars. Darker, more spread-out objects like nebulae or galaxies do not show visible levels of this distortion.

Webb doesn’t have a lens. Like all large telescopes, Webb uses a curved mirror to focus starlight. This mirror is located deep inside the telescope, protected by its long tube-like structure. As there is no atmosphere around Webb, there is no risk of dust or corrosion reaching inside.

With the retirement of NASA’s space shuttle fleet in 2011, there are no spacecraft currently in operation that could collect Webb and return it to Earth. Webb’s orbit is relatively stable, so it will not be immediately de-orbited when it stops working. However it will eventually need to be brought down in a controlled re-entry to ensure that any debris does not land in inhabited areas — Webb is too big to fully burn up in the atmosphere. During the final servicing mission in 2009, a docking device was fitted onto the base of the telescope so that a rocket can easily attach and safely de-orbit the telescope once its mission is complete.
